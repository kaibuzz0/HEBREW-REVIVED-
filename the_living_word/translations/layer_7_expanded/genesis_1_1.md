## Expanded Analysis

### [In [the] beginning [of all things]]

**בְּרֵאשִׁית - "In [the] beginning"**

The Hebrew בְּרֵאשִׁית (bǝrēʾšît) contains deliberate ambiguity:

**Option A: Absolute state** (Traditional)
- "In the beginning" - temporal starting point
- Similar to John 1:1 "In the beginning was the Word"
- MT vocalization supports this reading

**Option B: Construct state** (Alternative)
- "In the beginning of..." [implied: creation]
- "When God began to create..." (NJPS)
- Emphasizes process over absolute starting point

**Option C: Comprehensive** (This translation)
- "In the beginning [of all things]"
- Acknowledges both possibilities
- Brackets indicate supplied/implied words

**What we cannot know with certainty:**
- Whether the author intended absolute or construct
- Whether "beginning" refers to first moment or first phase
- Whether this answers "when" or "with what"

---

### [the Elohim [divine council/powers]]

**אֱלֹהִים - "Elohim"**

Critical expansion needed because modern translations flatten this to "God":

**Why "Elohim" and not "God":**

1. **Morphological marker:** Plural form (-im) is significant
   - Could be "plural of majesty" (intensive)
   - Could reflect divine council (cf. Psalm 82:1)
   - Could be vestige of polytheistic period
   - Never clearly explained in text

2. **Semantic range broader than "God":**
   - Used for Israel's God (2,300+ times)
   - Used for other gods (200+ times)
   - Used for angels/heavenly beings (rare)
   - Used for judges (rare, Psalm 82:6)

3. **Syntactic clues:** 
   - Takes plural verb? Sometimes (rare)
   - Usually takes singular verb (as here: "he created")
   - Suggests plural form with singular meaning

**Expanded options:**
- "the Elohim" - Preserves plural form
- "[divine council/powers]" - Notes possible meaning
- "God" - Too narrow, flattens semantic range

**What we cannot know:**
- Whether original audience heard "council" or "majesty"
- Whether plural is theological or grammatical
- How this relates to later Trinitarian doctrine

### [created [bara - divine-only verb]]

**בָּרָא - "he created"**

The verb בָּרָא is unique in Hebrew Bible:

**Exclusive usage:**
- 54 occurrences in Hebrew Bible
- **Always** with God as subject
- **Never** with human subject

**Contrast with other "making" verbs:**

| Hebrew | Meaning | Subject | Example |
|--------|---------|---------|---------|
| **בָּרָא** (bara) | create | God only | Genesis 1:1, 1:27 |
| **עָשָׂה** (asah) | make/do | God or human | Genesis 1:7, 2:15 |
| **יָצַר** (yatzar) | form/shape | God or human | Genesis 2:7, 2:19 |
| **כּוֹנֵן** (konen) | establish | God or human | Psalm 8:3 |

**Critical distinction:** Only בָּרָא indicates divine creative power

**What "created" implies:**
- Bringing into existence
- Absolute origin (possibly)
- No human analogue

**What "created" does NOT necessarily imply:**
- Creation ex nihilo (from nothing)
- Instantaneous creation
- Exclusion of pre-existing materials

**Debate:** Does בָּרָא mean:
- A. Create from nothing (ex nihilo)?
- B. Create order from chaos?
- C. Fill/fatten (etymological)?

Text alone cannot resolve this definitively.

### [the heavens [cosmic realm above]]

**הַשָּׁמַיִם - "the heavens"**

**Plural significance:**
- Dual form (ancient Hebrew had dual number)
- Possibly "upper and lower heavens"
- Or pluralis excellentiae (plural of majesty)

**Ancient conception:**
- Not modern "outer space"
- Cosmic waters above (separated by firmament)
- Realm of birds, clouds, sun, moon, stars
- Dwelling place of divine

**Translation options:**
- "the heavens" - Preserves plurality
- "the sky" - Too narrow
- "heaven" - Loses Hebrew form

---

### and the earth [terrestrial realm below]

**הָאָרֶץ - "the earth"**

**Semantic range:**
- Dry land (opposite of sea)
- Ground, soil
- Territory, country
- World, globe (modern understanding, not ancient)

**Ancient conception:**
- Flat disk
- Cosmic waters below
- Supported by pillars or foundations
- Under dome (firmament)

**Merism:** "Heavens and earth" = totality
- Pair of opposites representing everything
- Similar to "sun, moon, and stars" = all luminaries
- "High and low" = everywhere
- "Day and night" = always

**Translation note:** "Earth" preferred over "land" for cosmic scope

## Full Expanded Text

```
In [the] beginning [of all things, or: when God began to create], 
the Elohim [divine council/powers, or: God] 
created [bara - using divine-only verb, possibly: ordered pre-existing materials, or: brought into existence from nothing] 
the heavens [cosmic realm above, including firmament and waters above] 
and the earth [terrestrial realm below, dry land].
```

## What This Expansion Reveals

### Ambiguities Acknowledged:

1. **בְּרֵאשִׁית** - Absolute vs. construct state
2. **אֱלֹהִים** - Divine council vs. singular God
3. **בָּרָא** - Creation mechanism (ex nihilo vs. ordering)
4. **הַשָּׁמַיִם** - Exact cosmic scope
5. **הָאָרֶץ** - Earth vs. land

### Confidences:

| Element | Confidence | Reason |
|---------|------------|--------|
| "In the beginning" | **Probable** | MT vocalization supports |
| "Elohim" | **Directly attested** | Clear form |
| "created" | **Highly probable** | Divine-only verb |
| "heavens and earth" | **Directly attested** | Clear lexical equivalents |

### Debates Open:

1. **Theological:** Ex nihilo vs. creatio ex materia
2. **Historical:** Origins of Elohim plural
3. **Textual:** Absolute vs. construct reading
4. **Scientific:** Compatibility with cosmology

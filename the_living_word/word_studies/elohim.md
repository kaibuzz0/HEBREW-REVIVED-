# Divine Names Study: אֱלֹהִים (Elohim)

**Lemma:** אֱלֹהִים (elohim)  
**Root:** אלה (ʾlh)  
**Part of Speech:** Noun (plural form)  
**Occurrences:** ~2,600x in Hebrew Bible  

---

## Morphological Analysis

**Form:** אֱלֹהִים  
**Components:**
- Base: אֱלוֹהַּ (elowah) - deity/god
- Plural suffix: ־ים (-im)
- Reduced vowel: holem to holem-vav

**Plural Form, Singular Meaning?**

The form אֱלֹהִים is morphologically plural but takes singular verbs/adjectives when referring to the God of Israel. This creates interpretive complexity.

**Morphological Paradox:**
- Takes plural adjectives: "holy ones" (rare)
- Usually takes singular verbs: "he created" (bara, not beru)
- Can refer to multiple gods or single deity

---

## Semantic Range

### Primary Meanings

1. **The God of Israel (supreme deity)**
   - Genesis 1:1 - "Elohim created"
   - Psalm 19:1 - "The heavens declare the glory of Elohim"
   - Dominant usage (~2,300x)

2. **Divine beings, gods (plural)**
   - Exodus 12:12 - "gods of Egypt"
   - Psalm 82:1 - "Elohim stands in the congregation of El"
   - Can refer to angels, judges, other deities

3. **Divine council/heavenly assembly**
   - Psalm 82:6 - "I said, you are elohim"
   - 1 Kings 22:19 - "host of heaven standing around him"

4. **Angels/messengers**
   - Psalm 8:5 - "a little lower than elohim" (LXX: angels)
   - Job 1:6 - "sons of Elohim" (heavenly beings)

### Usage Distribution

| Meaning | Frequency | Examples |
|---------|-----------|----------|
| Israel's God | ~2,300 | Genesis 1:1, Exodus 20:1 |
| Other gods | ~200 | Exodus 12:12, Judges 11:24 |
| Angels/beings | ~50 | Psalm 8:5, Job 1:6 |
| Judges | ~5 | Exodus 21:6, Psalm 82:6 |

## Historical Usage Timeline

### Earliest Attestation

**Ugaritic:** ʾilm (gods)
- Ugaritic Baal cycle: "assembly of ʾilm"
- Canaanite pantheon context
- **Important:** Israelite usage differs from Canaanite polytheism

### Pre-monarchic Israel
- Early usage: plural referring to divine council
- Transitional: moving toward singular supreme deity
- **Shemot:** YHWH revealed as personal name

### Monarchic Period
- Jerusalem temple cult: YHWH as supreme
- Elohim as title/attribute
- Some scholars detect "convergence" (YHWH-Elohim identified)

### Exilic/Post-exilic
- Deutero-Isaiah: Elohim as universal God
- Genesis 1 (Priestly): Elohim as creator
- Monotheistic usage solidified

### Second Temple Period
- Elohim less personal, more abstract
- Philo: Elohim = logos (reason)
- Rabbinic: Elohim = attribute of justice (midrash)

### New Testament
- Greek: ὁ θεός (ho theos) - singular
- John: "the Word was with God [ton theon]"
- Paul: "there is no God but one" (1 Corinthians 8:4)

---

## Divine Council Theory

**Michael Heiser (The Unseen Realm):**
- Elohim in Genesis 1 refers to divine council
- "Let us make" (1:26) - council deliberation
- Not polytheism, but "monotheism with a divine council"

**Evidence:**
- Psalm 82:1 - "Elohim stands in the congregation of El"
- 1 Kings 22:19 - "host of heaven"
- Job 1-2 - "sons of Elohim"
- Deuteronomy 32:8-9 (Qumran) - "sons of Elohim" (MT: "sons of Israel")

**Confidence:** **Plausible** - Multiple texts suggest council imagery

## Ancient Translations

### Septuagint (LXX)
**ὁ θεός** (ho theos) - singular, definite article
- Treats אֱלֹהִים as singular proper noun
- No hint of plurality in translation

**Variations:**
- Sometimes κύριος (kyrios - Lord) when replacing YHWH
- ὁ θεὸς τῶν θεῶν (God of gods) - Deuteronomy 10:17

### Vulgate
**Deus** - singular
- Follows LXX precedent
- No attempt to preserve plural form

### Peshitta (Syriac)
**ܐܠܗܐ** (alaha) - singular
- Same as NT usage
- Standard term for God

### Targum Onkelos
**אֱלָהָא** (elaha) - Aramaic singular
- Adds suffix for definiteness
- Translates plural form to singular meaning

## Theological Interpretations

### Jewish Perspectives

**Classical Rabbinic:**
- **Plural of majesty:** Intensive plural, not numerical
- **Attribute of power:** Elohim = justice (vs. YHWH = mercy)
- **Midrash:** "I am the One who is called Elohim by all nations"

**Medieval:**
- **Maimonides:** Elohim indicates "cause of causes"
- **Nachmanides:** Names reveal different modes of divine operation

**Modern Jewish:**
- NJPS: "God" (flattened)
- Some academic: preserves "Elohim" in transliteration

### Christian Perspectives

**Patristic:**
- **Trinitarian reading:** Plural hints at Trinity
  - Augustine: "Let us make" = Father speaks to Son/Spirit
  - Caution: Anachronistic for original meaning

**Reformation:**
- **Luther:** "Gott" - functional translation
- **Calvin:** "God" - accepts traditional rendering

**Modern Critical:**
- **Scholars:** Preserve plural significance
- **Conservative:** Maintain "God" for familiarity

## Translation Decision

### Recommended Rendering

**Layer 2 (Transliteration):**
אֱלֹהִים (ʾĕlōhîm)

**Layer 6 (Literal):**
"Elohim" or "the Elohim"

**Layer 7 (Expanded):**
"the Elohim [divine powers/council]," or  
"the gods [understood as supreme deity in Israelite usage]"

### Rationale

1. **Preserve plural form:** Don't flatten to generic "God"
2. **Indicate semantic range:** Could be council, supreme deity, or both
3. **Historical context:** Pre-ex Israelites may have understood council imagery
4. **Avoid anachronism:** Don't read Trinity back into text
5. **Footnote ambiguity:** Explain interpretive options

### Confidence Classification

**Original Meaning:** **Probable** - Supreme deity in council context, but exact nuance unclear

**What Can Be Said with Confidence:**
- Plural form is significant and should not be flattened
- Refers to Israel's God in Genesis 1
- Possible allusion to divine council (1:26 "Let us")
- Not merely "plural of majesty" (simplistic)

---

## What Is Lost in "God"

### Lost in Translation:

1. **Plurality mystery:** Why plural form for singular deity?
2. **Council imagery:** Connection to Psalm 82, Job 1-2
3. **Cultural context:** Israelite polytheism-to-monotheism transition
4. **Textual connections:** Link to "gods of Egypt," "sons of God"
5. **Theological development:** How name usage changed over time

### What "Elohim" Preserves:

1. **Linguistic accuracy:** Actual form used
2. **Interpretive openness:** Allows multiple understandings
3. **Historical honesty:** Doesn't impose later theology
4. **Study invitation:** Encourages reader investigation
